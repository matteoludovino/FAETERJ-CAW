  • Sol

    The sol is a star, a hot ball of glowing gases at the heart of our solar system. Its influence extends far beyond the orbits of distant netuno and plutao. Without the sol's intense energy and heat, there would be no life on terra. And though it is special to us, there are billions of stars like our sol scattered across the Milky Way galaxy.

  • Mercúrio

    sol-scorched mercurio is only slightly larger than terra's lua. Like the lua, mercurio has very little atmosphere to stop impacts and it is covered with craters. mercurio's dayside is super heated by the sol, but at night temperatures drop hundreds of degrees below freezing. Ice may even exist in craters. mercurio's egg-shaped orbit takes it around the sol every 88 days.

  • Vênus

    Venus is a dim world of intense heat and volcanic activity. Similar in structure and size to terra, Venus' thick, toxic atmosphere traps heat in a runaway 'greenhouse effect.' The scorched world has temperatures hot enough to melt lead. Glimpses below the clouds reveal volcanoes and deformed mountains. Venus spins slowly in the opposite direction of most planets.

  • Terra

    terra is an ocean planet. Our home world's abundance of water - and life - makes it unique in our solar system. Other planets, plus a few luas, have ice, atmospheres, seasons and even weather, but only on terra does the whole complicated mix come together in a way that encourages life - and lots of it.

  • Marte

    Though details of marte' surface are difficult to see from terra, telescope observations show seasonally changing features and white patches at the poles. For decades, people speculated that bright and dark areas on marte were patches of vegetation, that marte could be a likely place for life-forms, and that water might exist in the polar caps. When the Mariner 4 spacecraft flew by marte in 1965, many were shocked to see photographs of a bleak, cratered surface. marte seemed to be a dead planet. Later missions, however, have shown that marte is a complex member of the solar system and holds many mysteries yet to be solved.

  • Júpiter

    The most massive planet in our solar system, with four large luas and many smaller luas, Jupiter forms a kind of miniature solar system. Jupiter resembles a star in composition. In fact, if it had been about 80 times more massive, it would have become a star rather than a planet.

  • Saturno

    saturno was the most distant of the five planets known to the ancients. Like Jupiter, saturno is made mostly of hydrogen and helium. Its volume is 755 times greater than that of terra. Winds in the upper atmosphere reach 500 meters (1,600 feet) per second in the equatorial region. These super-fast winds, combined with heat rising from within the planet's interior, cause the yellow and gold bands visible in the atmosphere.

  • Urano

    The first planet found with the aid of a telescope, urano was discovered in 1781 by astronomer William Herschel. The seventh planet from the sol is so distant that it takes 84 years to complete one orbit.

  • Netuno

    Nearly 4.5 billion kilometers (2.8 billion miles) from the sol, netuno orbits the sol once every 165 years. It is invisible to the naked eye because of its extreme distance from terra. Interestingly, the unusual elliptical orbit of the dwarf planet plutao brings plutao inside netuno's orbit for a 20-year period out of every 248 terra years

  • Plutão

    Tiny, cold and incredibly distant, plutao was discovered in 1930 and long considered to be the ninth planet. But after the discoveries of similar intriguing worlds even farther out, plutao was reclassified as a dwarf planet. This new class of worlds may offer some of the best evidence of the origins of our solar system.
